5 Office Space Tasks to Plan Before Going Back to Work

Getting ahead of employee concerns with these 5 plans

As companies look ahead to the future of work after the pandemic, they’re navigating uncharted waters. Businesses and employees are struggling to understand shifting state and local guidance, to adhere to regulations and, most of all, to make sure that workspaces are safe, healthy and ready to welcome people back into them. They’re also leveraging digital tools and technologies in new ways – to facilitate collaboration, to ensure capacity limits aren’t exceeded, and to keep people safe and secure – as they incorporate new protocols into daily office routines.

What considerations should employers keep in mind when planning the return to office after COVID?

1. Capacity Limits

Workplace reopening plans must adhere to local regulations, which are different in various cities, states and localities.

Many areas have lifted restrictions on many industries, but not all, and guidelines and best practices continue to evolve along with the science.

1. Capacity Limits

The best access control and access monitoring solutions are those that are flexibile and adaptable, since community transmission rates and local recommendations continue to change.

1. Capacity Limits

The CDC still recommends that people maintain six-foot distancing from others who are not in their household; OSHA recommends staggering work shifts, limiting breakroom capacity and putting measures in place to reduce the number of people who are in the same space at once.

Monitoring the total number of people in buildings as well as how they’re distributed within indoor spaces is now a best practice, because it makes it possible to adhere to CDC and OSHA recommendations

1. Capacity Limits

If you’re keeping a daily occupancy log that includes individual entry and exit histories, this simplifies contact.

Automated access counting systems make it easy to collect and monitor the necessary data.

Keep employees informed about how you will ensure their safety by limiting the total number of people on-site at any given time. This is key for earning and retaining their trust.

2. Health and Safety Screening

The CDC has issued updated guidance about which symptoms and combinations of symptoms are most likely to be associated with COVID-19 infection.

2. Health and Safety Screening

Screening workers for these symptoms lowers the risk that someone who’s highly infectious will be present on site.

In some industries and localities, employees may need to validate their vaccination or testing status to verify that they’re eligible to enter the workplace.

2. Health and Safety Screening

Daily temperature checks and/or symptom screenings can be requested through a mobile app-based attestation form that’s easy to use, convenient and quick.

Different industries and localities may have different symptom screening requirements. Choose a solution that’s customizable so that you can tailor questions to meet your enterprise’s individual needs.

2. Health and Safety Screening

Look for a solution that can also integrate testing or vaccination status validation into the HR system of record.

A solution that integrates with third-party health pass platforms makes it simple to verify and share this information in a manner that’s private and secure.

Using screening results and/or vaccination verification to inform your daily plan of action can help keep everyone in the organization healthy and safe.

3. Sanitizing Surfaces

Both the CDC and OSHA recommend that surfaces be cleaned and sanitized regularly. They also recommend that all employees be asked to follow handwashing protocols.

3. Sanitizing Surfaces

Cleaning and disinfecting surfaces in public spaces can reduce the risk of transmission of COVID-19 as well as other infectious diseases.

It’s recommended to use a surface virucidal disinfectant containing 0.05% sodium hypochlorite or at least 70% ethanol.

3. Sanitizing Surfaces

Replacing high-touch surfaces in common areas with touchless solutions further reduces the in-office risk to employees.

Areas where you should consider implementing touchless controls include entry and access, elevators and visitor management.

Reminding workers of no-touch adaptations in the workplace (as well as increase cleaning and sanitization procedures) can help increase their trust and confidence that they’ll remain safe as they return to work.

3. Sanitizing Surfaces

Many workplaces are adopting hybrid models that allow for more employees to work from home more often, and thus are using hot-desking or hoteling (temporarily assigned seating). Informing employees about sanitization procedures is especially important in these scenarios.

Your organization can integrate desk scheduling with access monitoring and sanitization scheduling solutions to ensure that shared workspaces are cleaned between times they’re occupied.

4. Improving Air Quality

COVID-19 spreads primarily through aerosols (tiny airborne particles and respiratory droplets).

4. Improving Air Quality

High-quality air filtration and increasing exchange rates with outdoor air can reduce the risks of transmitting infection within any indoor space.

Ventilation system upgrades or improvements can increase the delivery of clean air, decrease the presence of contaminants and decrease the concentration of viral particles in the air.

4. Improving Air Quality

Increase air filtration levels as high as is possible to do without reducing airflow.

Consider installing high-efficiency particulate air (HEPA) fan or filtration systems where possible, especially in higher-risk areas.

Portable CO2 monitors can be integrated with HVAC systems to provide information on whether or not target ventilation benchmarks are being achieved. The CDC considers 800 parts per million (ppm) evidence of good ventilation.

4. Improving Air Quality

Educate employees about the steps that you are taking to improve their safety by filtering indoor air and monitoring air quality.

Air quality reports can increase employees’ confidence as they return to work.

5. Transparent Communication

For many employees, the return to physical office spaces comes with mixed feelings.

Some are eager to reconnect with colleagues and co-workers in person; others are nervous about health risks at work.

5. Transparent Communication

Listen to employees’ concerns and take them seriously.

Share comprehensive plans and information about what you’re doing to mitigate risks. Communicate early and often.

5. Transparent Communication

Greater transparency builds trust.

Consider a multi-layered approach to increasing workplace safety that includes touchless access, daily occupant screening, contact tracing, testing or vaccination verification, capacity monitoring and air quality monitoring.
